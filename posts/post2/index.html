Robert Denoon Cumming on Sartrean Dialectics

2022-04-20

Robert Denoon Cumming’s article “This Place of Violence, Obscurity, and Witchcraft” (1979) is a hidden gem. Utterly bizarre in style, it presents Sartre’s understanding of dialectics really nicely, in almost point form.

The first thing, noted by Sartre himself, is that dialectics is less a systematic philosophy or explanation of how things work, but a way (methodos) of making things intelligible. The question is how.

Cumming starts on a point I find particularly seductive: the idea of dialectic as “a method of sustained definition.” As Cumming puts it:

Thus Plato’s Republic is a sustained attempt to define justice; Das Kapital, a sustained attempt to define capitalism. Those who employ undialectical methods usually get their definitions out of the way promptly and then settle down to what they regard as the real business of dealing with objective facts. (183)

Like both Plato and Marx, Sartre thinks dialectics is not really about constructing a new, a priori, or ahistorical definition. It’s about tracking and reconstructing “already available” definitions. At stake is a working-through of existing definitions – their criticism, the exposure of their internal contradictions and the lines of contradiction between them – that enables a shift from a world that actually is described in those definitions toward a world in which the ‘proper’ definition might emerge. Really dialectics is about working at the edge of the world in which we live and another world, a way of being otherwise. Dialectics as science, or as knowledge, is a process “involving a critical redefinition of the participants' preconceptions” (184).

Cumming argues that Sartre’s version of this critical reconception is crucially accelerated in an account he gives of a Frenchman seemingly conforming to Americanness before Sartre’s eyes.

Marx’s Das Kapital, as a dialectical attempt to define what capital is… is an analysis of the contradictions operating in the process of transformation whereby a capitalistic society must disintegrate in order to be integrated into its opposite – a socialist society. In the case of Sartre’s encounter with the Franco-American, the movements of disintegration and reintegration are accelerated, as if they were taking place right there in front of Sartre… rather than proceeding at what would be the historical pace of a melting-pot – the historical pace a Marxist analysis would respect. (189)

I guess what I would add here is that Sartre is intensifying something dialectical writing always displays: the compression of historical or philosophical processes through abbeviation, narration, and/or logical reconstruction. That is, in pointing to an acceleration, Cumming is noting – I think – the special form of abstraction that a dialectical analysis offers. It is not an abstraction that holds things still or ‘freezes’ them (Walter Benjamin’s idea of a “dialectical image” notwithstanding). Indeed, it does not slow down the world at all. Most of the processes that dialectical philosophy tracks (the movement from becoming to being to the kyrion agathon in Plato, the emergence and demise of capitalism in Marx) are very long and very slow. Thus dialectics always needs to speed things up to condense them, to help make intelligible their contradictions, their fits and starts, in a way amenable to practical intervention.

Crucially, this specific form of abbreviation stands in clear opposition to other ones. It certainly differs from abstraction modelled on mathematics and Newtonian physics: the reduction of the world to logical necessity, measuring the empirical by a sterile ideal – what Koyre called measuring the possible by the impossible. But it also differs from the ossification of metaphors into concepts. Metaphors need to be revived; indeed, Sartre considers Marxism just another dead metaphor: though itself “the outcome of a process of soical transformation… it has become deatched from the process. Its application to succeeding phases has become merely ‘ceremonial,’ like the application of a dead metaphor.” (197)

Dialectics, too, requires a process of “sustained redefinition.” It too, risks being made into an ossified metaphor, a hardened method, or an abstract system. It’s character as an accelerating mode of thought necessarily contains seductive but deleterious move toward the freezing of acceleration into image. Sartre’s rejection of “mechanistic explanation” (190) is helpful here, because he insists on the irreducibility of subjectivity in dialectical thought. While one shouldn’t go back to an idealist entrapment in the subject, nor should we displace dialectics to the ‘objective world’ as a feature of it. For me, the way to resist the temptation either way is precisely to study this moves of abbreviation, condensation, and acceleration, of which both Sartre and Cummings’s reading of him are exemplary.
